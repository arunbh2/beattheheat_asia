The Growing Threat of Very Warm Nights

Over the last decade, very warm nights have increased faster than very hot days, preventing the human body from cooling down after intense daytime heat.

This significantly increases health risks such as heat strokes and worsens non-communicable diseases like diabetes and hypertension.

Risk Assessment Framework

IPCC AR5 Methodology
H
Hazard: Heat intensity, humidity, frequency
E
Exposure: Population density, geographic distribution
V
Vulnerability: Socio-economic, health, infrastructure factors

Risk = f(Hazard × Exposure × Vulnerability)

Urban Heat Islands: Cities Under Siege

Cities experience temperatures 2-8°C higher than surrounding rural areas, with nighttime differences reaching up to 12°C.

UHI Contributors

🏢 Dense built environments trap and retain heat
🛣️ Concrete and asphalt absorb and retain heat
🏭 Industrial emissions and energy use generate waste heat
🌳 Reduced vegetation eliminates natural cooling
🚗 Vehicular emissions add to heat pollution

Critical Impact: With nearly 50% of India's population expected to live in urban areas by 2050, this poses a serious threat to public health and economic productivity.

The Human Face of Heat

Vulnerable communities who have historically contributed the least to climate change are disproportionately affected.

👩

Women & Children

Pregnant women and children are especially vulnerable. Heat affects pregnancy outcomes and child development, with informal sector women facing increased exposure in poorly ventilated work environments.

50M+ Women in informal heat-exposed work
👷

Outdoor Workers

Construction workers, farmers, delivery personnel, and street vendors face direct heat exposure. Up to 75% of India's workforce depends on heat-exposed labor, affecting 380 million people.

380M People in heat-exposed labor
👴

Elderly & Chronically Ill

Those with existing health conditions like diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease face increased risks. Heat exacerbates chronic conditions and limits access to cooling.

1.05B People exposed to extreme heat stress (2024)

The Dignity Deficit

The inability to live, work, and learn in thermally safe environments is fundamentally an issue of human dignity and equity. Access to cooling, safe working conditions, and resilient livelihoods in the face of extreme heat is a fundamental human right.

Economic & Environmental Costs

Heat stress threatens to derail development progress and exacerbate poverty across multiple sectors.

Labor Productivity Losses

34M Job losses projected in India by 2030 due to heat stress

Sector-Specific Impacts

  • 🌾 Agriculture: 47% of India's workforce facing heat stress
  • 🏗️ Construction: 11% of workforce working in direct sunlight
  • 🏭 Manufacturing: Up to 30% decline in factory output during summer
  • 📦 Delivery/Logistics: Workers spending 8-10 hours daily on roads

Agricultural & Energy Impacts

Crop Losses (2024)

Extreme weather events affected 3.2 million hectares of crop area in 2024.

Price Impacts in Haryana:

  • 🥒 Okra: ₹80 → ₹100/kg
  • 🍇 Grapes: ₹70 → ₹120/kg
  • 🥬 Green vegetables: +30% in 2 days

Temperature Impact: 1°C increase in nighttime temperatures reduces wheat yields by 6% and rice yields by up to 10%.

Energy Grid Strain

Extreme heat strains energy grids, damages infrastructure, and increases cooling demand exponentially.

Cascading Effects: Infrastructure damage, supply chain disruptions, increased healthcare costs, and reduced business productivity.

Global Leaders Speak

Authoritative voices frame extreme heat as a humanitarian crisis and matter of global justice.

"Extreme heat is increasingly tearing through economies, widening inequalities, undermining the Sustainable Development Goals and killing people. Heat is estimated to kill almost half a million people a year, that's about 30 times more than tropical cyclones."

— UN Secretary-General António Guterres, July 2024

"Extreme heat is the most visible effect of climate change, everyone is affected. Those with existing health conditions will be made much worse with extreme heat and it impacts the health of everyone in sometimes surprising ways. Even mental health is affected by heat, making people confused, anxious or even violent."

— Dr. Maria Neira, Director, Department of Environment, Climate Change and Health, WHO, July 2024

"Human-caused climate change is already affecting many weather and climate extremes in every region across the globe. This has led to widespread adverse impacts and related losses and damages to nature and people. Vulnerable communities who have historically contributed the least to current climate change are disproportionately affected."

— IPCC AR6 Synthesis Report, Headline Statements
